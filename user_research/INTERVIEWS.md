# Target Users: Needs, Challenges & Behaviors

## ðŸ‘¥ Who Are Our Target Users?

Individuals aged 29-73 managing chronic health conditions (diabetes, hypertension, arthritis, autoimmune disorders, respiratory diseases) who take 3-9 daily medications and see healthcare providers 4-12+ times per year.

---

## ðŸŽ¯ Primary User Needs

### 1. Medication Management Support
- **Need:** Reliable reminders to take medications on time
- **Need:** Confirmation that medication was already taken (avoid double-dosing)
- **Need:** Clear instructions for complex timing (with food, 30 min before meals, etc.)
- **Need:** Visual identification of similar-looking medications
- **Need:** Tracking as-needed medications alongside regular schedules

### 2. Appointment Coordination
- **Need:** Timely appointment reminders with preparation info
- **Need:** Proactive reminders to schedule follow-up appointments
- **Need:** Easy coordination of multiple specialist appointments
- **Need:** Calendar integration to avoid scheduling conflicts

### 3. Healthcare Communication
- **Need:** Centralized medication list to share with multiple providers
- **Need:** Secure messaging with healthcare team
- **Need:** Easy way to export health data for doctor visits

### 4. Accessibility & Usability
- **Need:** Large text and high contrast for visual impairments
- **Need:** Voice commands for hands-free operation
- **Need:** Simplified interface for cognitive difficulties ("brain fog")
- **Need:** Large touch targets for motor impairments (tremors, arthritis)

### 5. Privacy & Security
- **Need:** Confidence that health data is secure and private
- **Need:** Control over who can access their health information
- **Need:** HIPAA compliance and transparent privacy policies

### 6. Caregiver Support
- **Need:** Ability to share information with family/caregivers
- **Need:** Maintain independence while receiving support
- **Need:** Permission-based access controls

---

## ðŸ˜° Primary User Challenges

### Medication Adherence Challenges
- **76% forget to take medications** at least sometimes
- **Confusion about timing:** "Did I already take this?" is a daily question
- **Complex schedules:** Multiple medications with different timing rules
- **Similar medications:** Can't distinguish between look-alike pills
- **As-needed tracking:** Difficult to remember when rescue inhalers or pain meds were used

### Appointment Management Challenges
- **Scheduling conflicts:** Coordinating work, family, and multiple doctor appointments
- **Forgetting follow-ups:** Remember the appointment exists, but forget to schedule it
- **Rural access:** 2-hour drives to specialists require careful planning
- **Preparation:** Don't know what to bring or what questions to ask

### Technology Challenges
- **Notification fatigue:** Existing apps send too many alerts, leading to abandonment
- **Complexity overload:** Apps with too many features are overwhelming
- **Small text/buttons:** Hard to read and interact with standard interfaces
- **Learning curve:** 44% are basic/beginner users who struggle with new apps

### Communication Challenges
- **Fragmented care:** Multiple providers who don't communicate with each other
- **Repeating information:** Constantly re-explaining medication lists and symptoms
- **Hard to reach doctors:** Need easier way to ask questions between appointments

### Physical & Cognitive Challenges
- **Visual impairments:** 32% need larger text and high contrast
- **Motor impairments:** 16% have difficulty with touch controls (tremors, arthritis)
- **Cognitive impairments:** 12% need simplified interfaces (brain fog, memory issues)
- **"Bad days":** Need app to work even when feeling worst

---

## ðŸ” Key User Behaviors

### Current Medication Management Behaviors
- **64% rely on memory/routine** (often fails)
- **56% use pill organizers** (but still forget if they took pills)
- **44% use phone alarms** (but ignore them after a while)
- **32% depend on family reminders** (want more independence)
- **24% have tried medication apps** (but abandoned due to notification fatigue)

### Current Appointment Tracking Behaviors
- **40% use digital calendars** (but manual entry is tedious)
- **28% use paper calendars** (but easy to double-book)
- **20% rely on memory alone** (frequently miss appointments)
- **8% use provider portals** (but fragmented across multiple systems)

### Technology Comfort Levels
- **20% are expert users** - Use many apps daily, want advanced features
- **36% are comfortable users** - Use apps regularly, want balanced functionality
- **32% are basic users** - Use simple apps only, need clear instructions
- **12% are beginners** - Struggle with new apps, need extensive support

### Privacy Behaviors
- **72% are very/extremely concerned** about health data privacy
- Want biometric authentication and two-factor authentication
- Need granular control over what data is shared and with whom
- Prefer apps with clear, transparent privacy policies

### Support-Seeking Behaviors
- **32% rely on family members** for medication management support
- Want to maintain independence while receiving help
- Need caregivers to see schedules without losing autonomy
- Prefer permission-based sharing (user controls what caregiver sees)

---

## ðŸ’¡ User Motivations

### What Motivates Users to Manage Their Health?
1. **Fear of health consequences** - Missing medications can lead to hospitalization
2. **Desire for independence** - Want to manage health without burdening family
3. **Quality of life** - Staying healthy means staying active and engaged
4. **Family responsibilities** - Need to be healthy for children, grandchildren
5. **Trust in healthcare team** - Want to follow doctor's recommendations

### What Frustrates Users About Current Solutions?
1. **Too many notifications** - Apps that spam alerts get deleted
2. **Overly complex** - Features they don't need make apps confusing
3. **Poor accessibility** - Small text and buttons are unusable
4. **Unreliable** - Apps that crash or miss reminders lose trust
5. **Privacy concerns** - Unclear data practices cause abandonment

# Research Methodology

## Semi-Structured Interviews (Qualitative)

**Target:** 10 participants with chronic health conditions  
**Duration:** 30-45 minutes per interview  
**Format:** Video calls  
**Research Period:** February 2026

### Purpose
- Deep insights into user behaviors and motivations
- Understand emotional aspects of health management
- Explore pain points in detail
- Gather rich contextual information

### Participant Recruitment

**Inclusion Criteria:**
- Age 18+ years
- Diagnosed with at least one chronic health condition
- Takes regular medication
- Owns a smartphone
- Willing to share health management experiences

**Diversity Achieved:**
- **Age range:** 29-73 years
- **Gender:** 6 Female, 4 Male
- **Tech proficiency:** 3 Beginner, 4 Intermediate, 3 Advanced
- **Locations:** Urban, Suburban, Rural
- **Conditions:** 10+ different chronic conditions

## Interview Questions

### Background (5 minutes)

**Q1:** Can you tell me about your health condition(s) and how long you've been managing it?

**Q2:** Walk me through a typical day managing your healthâ€”what does that look like?

### Medication Management (10 minutes)

**Q3:** Tell me about the medications you take. How many and how often?

**Q4:** How do you remember to take your medications?

**Q5:** Can you describe a time when you forgot to take your medication? What happened?

**Q6:** What's the most frustrating part about managing your medications?

**Q7:** Do you track when you take your medications? If so, how?

### Appointment Management (8 minutes)

**Q8:** How often do you see doctors or healthcare providers?

**Q9:** How do you keep track of your appointments?

**Q10:** Have you ever missed an appointment? What happened?

**Q11:** What's challenging about scheduling and managing appointments?

### Technology Usage (7 minutes)

**Q12:** What devices do you use daily? (smartphone, tablet, computer)

**Q13:** How comfortable are you with using mobile apps?

**Q14:** Have you ever used any health or medication apps? What was your experience?

**Q15:** What would make a health app easy or difficult for you to use?

### Needs & Concerns (7 minutes)

**Q16:** What motivates you to stay on top of your medication schedule?

**Q17:** What concerns would you have about using a health management app?

**Q18:** If you could design the perfect health app, what would it do?

### Closing (2 minutes)

**Q19:** Is there anything else about managing your health that we haven't talked about but you think is important?

# Interview Responses

## Participant 1: Margaret Chen

**Profile:**
- **Age:** 68 | **Gender:** Female
- **Condition:** Type 2 Diabetes, Hypertension
- **Medications:** 5 daily medications
- **Tech Proficiency:** Beginner | **Location:** Suburban

**Key Insights:**

*Medication Management:*
> "I use a pill organizer that my daughter fills for me every Sunday. But sometimes I forget if I already took my morning pills or not. I'll stand there staring at the organizer wondering, 'Did I take these already?' It's scary because with my blood pressure medication, you can't double up."

**Pain Points:** Confusion about whether medications were already taken, difficulty reading small text, needs larger fonts and high contrast, relies heavily on family support

*Appointment Management:*
> "I write everything in my wall calendar in the kitchen. But sometimes I schedule two appointments on the same day by accident because I forget to check."

**Ideal Features:** Large clear text, simple yes/no confirmations, voice reminders, easy way to share information with daughter

**Quote:** *"I don't want something complicated. I just want to know: Did I take my pills? When is my next appointment? That's it."*

## Participant 2: James Rodriguez

**Profile:**
- **Age:** 42 | **Gender:** Male
- **Condition:** Crohn's Disease, Anxiety
- **Medications:** 4 daily medications + as-needed
- **Tech Proficiency:** Advanced | **Location:** Urban

**Key Insights:**

*Medication Management:*
> "I've tried probably six different medication apps. They all start out great, but then the notifications become overwhelming. I get a notification to take my pill, then a notification asking if I took it, then a reminder that I didn't mark it as taken. It's like, I get it! I'm trying to live my life here."

**Ideal Features:** Smart notifications that learn his patterns, symptom tracking with medication correlation, direct integration with doctor's scheduling system, data visualization

**Quote:** *"I want an app that works WITH me, not against me. Learn my routine, don't just bombard me with reminders."*

## Participant 3: Patricia Johnson

**Profile:**

## Key Themes from Interviews

### Top Pain Points
1. **Medication Adherence** (10/10 participants) - Forgetting doses, confusion about timing
2. **Appointment Coordination** (9/10 participants) - Scheduling conflicts, multiple specialists
3. **Accessibility Needs** (6/10 participants) - Visual, motor, or cognitive limitations
4. **Notification Fatigue** (3/10 participants) - Too many alerts from existing apps
5. **Fragmented Systems** (7/10 participants) - Multiple apps, no integration

### Top Desired Features
1. **Smart Medication Reminders** (10/10 participants)
2. **Easy Medication Tracking** (9/10 participants)
3. **Accessibility Features** (6/10 participants) - Large text, voice commands
4. **Appointment Management** (9/10 participants)
5. **Provider Communication** (8/10 participants)

### User Segments Identified
1. **Tech-Savvy Data Seekers** (30%) - James, David, Sarah
2. **Simplicity Seekers** (30%) - Margaret, Linda, Robert
3. **Busy Balancers** (40%) - Patricia, Michael, Emily, Thomas, Sarah
